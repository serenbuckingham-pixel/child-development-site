Child and Young Person Development Milestones

Birth – 1 Year

Physical Development

During the first year, babies gradually develop control over their bodies and begin basic movements that lead to independence.

  • Head control – by around 3 months, a baby can lift and hold their head while on their tummy.
  • Rolling over – by 6 months, babies can roll from front to back and back to front.
  • Sitting independently – most babies can sit without support by 8 months.
  • Pulling to stand – by 12 months, babies can hold furniture and stand upright.

Speech, Language & Communication

Babies start developing communication skills, including sounds, responses, and first words.

  • Cooing and babbling – around 3–6 months, babies make repeated sounds like “ba” or “da.”
  • Responding to words – babies may react to their name or familiar voices.
  • First words – simple words like “mama” or “dada” usually appear by 12 months.

Cognitive Development

Babies begin to understand their environment and explore how things work.

  • Recognising familiar faces – babies distinguish between familiar and unfamiliar people.
  • Exploring objects – using hands and mouth to investigate toys.
  • Curiosity – showing interest in new objects and sounds, experimenting with cause and effect.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Babies form early attachments and show basic emotions.

  • Smiling in response – babies smile back when someone smiles at them.
  • Attachment – shows preference for parents or primary caregivers.
  • Expressing emotions – joy, anger, and fear are noticeable.

1 – 3 Years (Toddlers)

Physical Development

Toddlers refine their movements and begin performing everyday tasks independently.

  • Walking independently – by 18 months, most toddlers walk confidently.
  • Running and climbing – coordination and balance improve by 2 years.
  • Using utensils – toddlers begin feeding themselves with spoons and forks.

Speech, Language & Communication

Language develops rapidly, allowing toddlers to communicate wants and ideas.

  • Simple sentences – by 2 years, toddlers may say “I want juice.”
  • Following instructions – can understand and act on simple requests.
  • Vocabulary growth – naming familiar objects, people, and actions.

Cognitive Development

Toddlers begin exploring, problem-solving, and learning through play.

  • Imaginative play – using toys to imitate daily activities.
  • Understanding concepts – recognising colours, shapes, and sizes.
  • Problem-solving – figuring out simple puzzles or toy mechanisms.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Toddlers start to assert independence and develop social awareness.

  • Self-care – starts washing hands and feeding themselves with guidance.
  • Parallel play – plays alongside other children and begins to share.
  • Expressing feelings – shows happiness, frustration, or excitement clearly.

3 – 5 Years (Preschool)

Physical Development

Children in preschool develop coordination, strength, and fine motor skills for self-care and play.

  • Gross motor skills – running, hopping, jumping with balance.
  • Fine motor skills – drawing shapes, cutting with scissors, manipulating small objects.
  • Self-care – dressing and feeding independently.

Speech, Language & Communication

Language skills improve, supporting storytelling and understanding others.

  • Full sentences – uses sentences with correct grammar.
  • Understanding grammar – plurals, tenses, and pronouns correctly.
  • Storytelling – describing events, retelling stories, enjoying rhymes.

Cognitive Development

Thinking becomes logical and imaginative, preparing children for school learning.

  • Counting and recognising numbers – understands basic maths.
  • Sorting – groups objects by colour, size, or shape.
  • Imaginative play – role-playing and pretending scenarios.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Preschoolers develop social skills, independence, and understanding of emotions.

  • Friendships – forms bonds and enjoys group play.
  • Understanding rules – follows routines and instructions.
  • Expressing empathy – recognises feelings in others.

5 – 11 Years (Primary School)

Physical Development

Children refine coordination, participate in organised activities, and develop strength.

  • Gross motor skills – plays sports, improves agility and endurance.
  • Fine motor skills – writes neatly, draws detailed pictures.
  • Hand-eye coordination – participates in games and activities requiring precision.

Speech, Language & Communication

Language becomes more advanced, supporting learning and social interaction.

  • Complex sentences – explains ideas clearly and in detail.
  • Reading and writing – reads fluently and writes coherent sentences.
  • Understanding figurative language – recognises jokes, idioms, and humour.

Cognitive Development

Children develop logical thinking, problem-solving, and curiosity about the world.

  • Critical thinking – solves problems and makes decisions independently.
  • Understanding cause and effect – links actions to outcomes.
  • Exploring interests – asks questions and investigates topics of interest.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Children build confidence, social skills, and a sense of responsibility.

  • Friendships – develops close bonds and cooperates with others.
  • Self-identity – recognises strengths, preferences, and interests.
  • Responsibility – follows rules, understands consequences, and contributes to group activities.

12 – 19 Years (Teenagers / Secondary School)

Physical Development

Teenagers experience puberty, growth, and increased physical capabilities.

  • Puberty – physical changes in body shape, voice, and sexual maturity.
  • Growth spurts – rapid height and weight gain.
  • Refined strength and coordination – supports sports and daily activity.

Speech, Language & Communication

Communication becomes more sophisticated, supporting academic and social life.

  • Expressing complex ideas – discusses opinions and abstract concepts.
  • Debating skills – can evaluate arguments and explain reasoning.
  • Digital communication – uses technology and media effectively.

Cognitive Development

Teenagers think critically and develop personal perspectives.

  • Critical thinking – analyses situations and solves complex problems.
  • Future planning – sets goals for education, career, or personal growth.
  • Abstract thought – understands hypothetical situations and moral reasoning.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Teenagers build independence, strong relationships, and emotional awareness.

  • Self-identity – develops individuality, values, and beliefs.
  • Relationships – forms close friendships and romantic connections.
  • Emotional regulation – manages stress, conflict, and complex feelings.
