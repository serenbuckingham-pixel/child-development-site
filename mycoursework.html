Child and Young Person Development Milestones

0 – 3 Years

Physical Development

Toddlers refine their movements and may begin to perform everyday tasks independently.

  • Some examples of these movements are lifting their head, rolling over, sitting up, climbing, crawling, and walking.

    • They may also learn to pass toys between their hands, move their arms up and down, and learn to use a pincer grasp.

    They may also learn to hold a pencil, kick a ball, or balance on one foot.

Speech, Language & Communication

Language develops rapidly, allowing toddlers to communicate their wants and needs.

  • They may begin responding to sounds, voices, and familiar noises (such as pets).
  • They might start to babble, coo, or make different sounds to express different needs.
  • They may begin to understand simple words and phrases.
  • They may say their first words and gradually build up their vocabulary.
  • They may also start to ask questions using simple words, such as ("Why?").
  • They may begin to follow simple instructions, and respond to requests.

Cognitive Development

Toddlers may begin to explore, problem-solve, and learn through play. They may also learn to:

  • Recognise familiar faces and voices.
  • Show curiosity and explore their surroundings.
  • Learn cause-and-effect (such as shaking or dropping things).
  • Begin to understand object permanence, through games like peekaboo.
  • Understand simple words, commands, or gestures.
  • Use basic gestures, words, and possibly short sentences to communicate.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Toddlers may start to assert independence and start to develop social awareness.

  • They may form a strong attachment to their primary caregiver, and seek comfort from them.
  • They may also begin to show a range of emotions such as joy, anger, sadness, frustration, or fear.
  • They may begin to show interest in other children, and may start playing alongside them rather than with them.
  • They may begin to follow simple rules and routines, with guidance from a primary caregiver.
  • They may begin to learn how to manage their emotions with adult support (such as calming down after being upset).
    • They may start to enjoy social interaction. This can mean they might begin to smile while playing with other people.

3 – 5 Years

Physical Development

Children in this age range begin to develop coordination, strength, and fine motor skills for self-care and play.

  • Children may learn to run, jump, climb, and throw with better coordination.
  • Children may learn to dress themselves with minimal help from their primary caregivers.
  • They may also learn to use utensils in order to eat more skillfully.
  • They may learn to draw basic shapes and lines, and may start to copy letters.
  • They may begin to show improved hand-eye coordination.

    • Speech, Language & Communication

    Language skills improve, supporting storytelling and understanding others.

    • They may begin to speak in full sentences, possibly 4-5 words or more.
    • They may begin telling simple stories or describing events in order.
    • Children may also use language to explain their ideas, feelings, and needs.
    • Children might also be able to understand more complex instructions.
    • They may also engage in back-and-forth conversations, and begin to ask questions.
    • They might also begin to use grammar correctly, and show a rapid expand in vocabulary.

    Cognitive Development

    Thinking starts to become logical and imaginative, preparing children for school learning.

    • Children may begin to solve simple problems and puzzles.
    • Children may begin to sort and group objects by more than one attribute (such as colour, shape, and size).
    • They may begin to engage in imaginative and pretend play with different roles and stories (such as playing house or doctors).
    • Children might begin to understand basic concepts of time, numbers, and sequences.

    Personal, Social & Emotional Development

    Children at this age begin to develop social skills, independence, and understanding of their own emotions.

    • Children may begin to show empathy, and may be able to understand other people's feelings better.
    • They may learn to play more cooperatively with other children, and are more likely to share.
    • They may express a wider range of emotions, and begin to learn ways that they can manage them.
    • Children may develop independence when performing daily tasks.
    • They may become better at negotiating and solving conflicts with their peers, with guidance.

5 – 11 Years (Primary School)

Physical Development

Children refine coordination, participate in organised activities, and develop strength.

  • Gross motor skills – plays sports, improves agility and endurance.
  • Fine motor skills – writes neatly, draws detailed pictures.
  • Hand-eye coordination – participates in games and activities requiring precision.

Speech, Language & Communication

Language becomes more advanced, supporting learning and social interaction.

  • Complex sentences – explains ideas clearly and in detail.
  • Reading and writing – reads fluently and writes coherent sentences.
  • Understanding figurative language – recognises jokes, idioms, and humour.

Cognitive Development

Children begin to develop logical thinking, problem-solving, and curiosity about the world.

  • They may begin to think logically about situations and problems.
  • They may begin to further understand cause-and-effect.
  • They can begin to understand rules and strategies in games.
  • They may also begin to focus on tasks for longer periods and remember more specific details.
  • They may also begin learning to read, write, and do basic maths.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Children may build confidence, social skills, and a sense of responsibility.

  • Children may develop stronger friendships and a stronger understanding of group dynamics.

12 – 19 Years (Teenagers / Secondary School)

Physical Development

Teenagers experience puberty, growth, and increased physical capabilities.

  • Puberty – physical changes in body shape, voice, and sexual maturity.
  • Growth spurts – rapid height and weight gain.
  • Refined strength and coordination – supports sports and daily activity.

Speech, Language & Communication

Communication becomes more sophisticated, supporting academic and social life.

  • Expressing complex ideas – discusses opinions and abstract concepts.
  • Debating skills – can evaluate arguments and explain reasoning.
  • Digital communication – uses technology and media effectively.

Cognitive Development

Teenagers think critically and develop personal perspectives.

  • Critical thinking – analyses situations and solves complex problems.
  • Future planning – sets goals for education, career, or personal growth.
  • Abstract thought – understands hypothetical situations and moral reasoning.

Personal, Social & Emotional Development

Teenagers build independence, strong relationships, and emotional awareness.

  • Self-identity – develops individuality, values, and beliefs.
  • Relationships – forms close friendships and romantic connections.
  • Emotional regulation – manages stress, conflict, and complex feelings.
